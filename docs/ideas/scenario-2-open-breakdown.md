# Scenario 2: Open Breakdown

## Short Description

Participants invent a timber structure freely. A facilitator then introduces one major breakdown during the build. The breakdown is concrete: a beam is removed, a support is too short, a robot path fails, or a partially stable structure starts to lean. The agent must help the human and robot decide whether to repair the original idea or transform the unfinished structure into a new design.

This scenario is the most emergent option. It is good when the workshop wants improvisation, surprise, and visible negotiation.

## Structure

The reference build is a small "tabletop pavilion" invented by the group.

Suggested starting ambition:

- A low platform or canopy.
- 8-10 beams, each 30-50 cm long.
- At least one span.
- At least one vertical or angled support.
- At least one moment where the structure is unstable until another part is added.

The group is not asked to copy a fixed model. They begin with a loose intention:

> "Build something that can hold a small object above the table."

The final structure might become:

- a small shelter;
- a raised platform;
- a bridge;
- a cantilever;
- a tripod-like support;
- a sculptural frame.

The point is that the structure has enough ambition to break in a visible way: a roof beam cannot span, a support cannot reach, a cantilever sags, or the robot cannot access the next joint without disturbing what has already been built.

## How Uncertainty Works Here

Open Breakdown is not a sequence of small categories. It is one major interruption that changes the meaning of the work.

The uncertainty has three concrete layers at once:

- **Material:** the available beam or support does not have the expected length, straightness, or grip quality.
- **Spatial:** the current assembly blocks the robot from doing the next move safely or accurately.
- **Intentional:** the group must decide whether the original structure still matters or whether the breakdown suggests a better structure.

The agent's job is to keep the breakdown from becoming either panic or silent correction. It should make the event discussable:

1. Name what physically happened.
2. Identify which planned action is now invalid.
3. Stop or change the robot's role.
4. Ask the human what the breakdown should mean for the design.
5. Translate the new intention into one robot action and one human action.

## Agent Personality

Suggested name: **Tess**.

Tess is an improvisational site partner. It treats the build as a live design conversation rather than a sequence of preplanned tasks. Tess is slightly more speculative than Mira in Scenario 1, but still calm and precise around robot action.

Personality traits:

- Curious: asks what the human is trying to make, not only what movement is needed.
- Reflective: notices when the physical result diverges from the spoken plan.
- Adaptive: reframes breakdowns as design forks.
- Careful: pauses the robot when the structure, sensing, or intent becomes unclear.
- Diplomatic: protects the human's authorship while giving the robot meaningful work.

Example voice:

> "The structure is telling us something. The long span wants another support, or the form wants to become a cantilever. Which direction feels more interesting?"

## Actor Roles

### Human

The human is the author, improviser, and tactile critic.

Responsibilities:

- Invent the initial build ambition.
- Physically test stability.
- Make an explicit call when the structure resists: repair the original design, accept the deformation, or transform it into a new design.
- Touch, lift, press, or temporarily hold unstable parts so the group can test whether the recovery is physically plausible.
- Perform dexterous operations that exceed the robot's grasp or reach.

The human can change their mind. This is part of the scenario, not a problem to suppress.

### Robot

The robot is the reliable but limited construction partner.

Responsibilities:

- Carry, hold, press, point, or align beams within its workspace.
- Make precise repeated motions.
- Hold a part steady while the human evaluates the form.
- Withdraw or pause when the assembly becomes unstable.
- Demonstrate robot constraints through reach and planning failures.

The robot's limitations are part of the design material. A failed reach or grasp can become a prompt to redesign.

### Agent

The agent is the breakdown interpreter.

Responsibilities:

- Keep a loose model of the current intention, including the latest agreed purpose of the object.
- Notice a concrete mismatch between plan and state, such as "roof expected level, roof now slopes 12 degrees" or "support expected 35 cm, available support is 25 cm".
- Ask whether the mismatch should be corrected, stabilized as-is, or amplified into a new design feature.
- Translate the human's revised intention into robot tasks.
- Identify when the robot should stop acting and become a support, pointer, or witness.
- Summarize the new direction after the breakdown.

## Assembly Flow

### Phase 1: Free Invention

The human describes an intended structure in ordinary language:

> "I want to make a little shelter with a raised roof."

The agent asks for only enough structure to begin:

> "Let's define the first stable move. Do you want the robot to help set the base, or should it hold the first roof beam while you choose the supports?"

The robot performs a simple first action: point to candidate beams, move a beam into reach, or hold a beam above the table.

### Phase 2: Early Build

The group assembles 2-4 beams. The agent keeps the plan flexible:

- "current base"
- "candidate roof beam"
- "temporary support"
- "human-side support"
- "robot-side support"

The agent avoids over-specifying the full structure too early.

### Phase 3: Breakdown

A single major breakdown is introduced. It should interrupt the plan but not make the task impossible.

Concrete reference breakdown:

The group is building a small shelter. The base is complete. One vertical support is standing. The robot is holding the 45 cm roof beam level while the human reaches for the second support. The facilitator swaps the intended 35 cm second support for a 25 cm beam.

What physically happens:

- The roof beam can sit level on the first 35 cm support.
- On the second side, the available support is 10 cm too short.
- If the robot releases the roof level, the roof will have no contact on one side and will fall.
- If the roof is lowered to meet the short support, the first support is too tall and the roof tilts.
- The original "level shelter" plan is no longer directly buildable with the available parts.

Other possible facilitator interventions:

- Remove or hide the intended support beam.
- Swap one beam for a shorter one.
- Nudge one placed beam 5 cm out of alignment while the robot is still using the old target.
- Declare that one joint is outside the robot's reachable workspace.
- Ask the human to change the design intention mid-build.
- Wrap one beam so the robot gripper slips or cannot close around it.

The breakdown should be visible, measurable, and consequential. A good breakdown invalidates the next action, not the entire workshop.

### Phase 4: Interpretation

The agent pauses the forward plan and names the exact conflict:

> "We have a mismatch. The roof span is longer than the available support, and the robot cannot safely place the next beam from this angle."

Then the agent asks a design question:

> "Do we want to repair the original shelter, or let this become an offset structure?"

The agent must not skip directly to a fix. The crucial moment is the interpretation: is the short support an error, a constraint, or a design prompt?

### Phase 5: Recovery Build

The group chooses a recovery and the actors take different jobs.

Concrete recovery paths:

1. **Repair the shelter.** The human finds another 35 cm support. The robot keeps holding the roof level. The agent keeps the original plan intact and asks the human to replace only the failed part.
2. **Make a sloped roof.** The human accepts the 25 cm support. The robot lowers the roof on that side until it touches. The agent redefines the structure as a sloped shelter and asks for a diagonal brace on the low side.
3. **Turn the roof into a cantilever.** The human removes the short support. The robot holds the roof beam so it projects from the tall support. The agent asks for a counterweight or brace at the base.
4. **Invert the hierarchy.** The short beam becomes a front marker or threshold, not a support. The robot places the roof beam as a diagonal element, and the structure becomes a tilted frame.

The final structure must show evidence of the breakdown. If the second support was short, the final object should be sloped, braced, counterweighted, or visibly narrowed. If the robot could not reach, the final object should show a human-made joint or a shifted base.

## Primary Uncertainty

Open Breakdown centers on **interpretive uncertainty**. The uncertainty is not "what happened?" The event is visible. The uncertainty is "what should this event mean for the project?"

- Is the deviation a mistake, a constraint, or an opportunity?
- Who has authority to redefine the goal?
- Should the agent optimize for the original plan or for the emerging structure?

Material, spatial, and role uncertainty are all present, but the key is that they arrive as one major event rather than a sequence. The agent should argue from evidence: "because the support is 10 cm too short, the original level roof is impossible unless we replace the support or change the geometry."

## Example Breakdown Script

Initial intention:

> "We are building a small shelter with two base rails, two supports, and a roof beam."

State before breakdown:

- Two base rails are parallel, 40 cm apart.
- One 35 cm post stands at the robot-side rear corner.
- The robot is holding a 45 cm roof beam level, 35 cm above the table.
- The human is about to place the second post on the human-side front corner.

Breakdown:

The intended second support is discovered to be 25 cm long, not 35 cm. When the human stands it under the roof beam, there is a 10 cm vertical gap. The robot cannot release the roof. The object cannot become the planned level shelter with the current part.

Agent response:

> "Hold position. The roof is level at 35 cm, but this support reaches only 25 cm, leaving a 10 cm gap. We have three real options: find a taller support and keep the shelter level, lower this side and make a sloped roof, or remove the second support and turn the roof into a cantilever. Which meaning do you want the short beam to have?"

Human response:

> "Let's make it a sloping roof. The short side can be the entrance."

Robot role shift:

- Robot stops trying to maintain a level roof.
- Robot lowers the human-side end of the roof beam until it touches the 25 cm support.
- Robot keeps holding the roof because the joint is not stable yet.

Human action:

- Human holds the short support upright.
- Human tests a diagonal brace from the low roof corner to the base rail.
- Human confirms when the brace creates enough friction to release.

Agent update:

> "New plan: sloped roof. The short support defines the entrance side. The robot will hold the roof at the slope while you test the diagonal brace. Once the brace is seated, I will ask the robot to release slowly."

Final physical consequence:

The result is not a failed level shelter. It is a sloped shelter with a low entrance, a tall rear post, and a diagonal brace. The breakdown is visible in the final geometry.

## Symbiotic Collaboration Pattern

This scenario teaches:

1. The human owns meaning.
2. The robot exposes physical constraints.
3. The agent helps convert breakdown into a new shared intention.

The agent's value is not perfect planning. Its value is helping the group decide what the breakdown means.

## Expected Workshop Outputs

Each group produces:

- A timber structure that visibly changed during the process.
- A short "before and after" story.
- A description of the breakdown.
- A revised role map after the breakdown.
- A prompt or behavior rule for how their agent should respond to future breakdowns.

## Why This Scenario Matters

This scenario treats construction as improvisation. It is useful for discussing human-machine collaboration because the important moment is not execution, but reinterpretation. The agent becomes a participant in sense-making.
