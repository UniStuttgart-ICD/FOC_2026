# Scenario 3 Expanded: Act, Agent Attributes, and Construction Problem Cards

## Core Idea

The workshop becomes a collaborative construction act.

Participants do not only build an object. They perform a short human-robot-agent workflow where:

- the **agent** has drawn characteristics it must fulfill;
- the **act/goal** defines the abstract artifact to fabricate;
- the **construction problem cards** introduce concrete uncertainty during the build.

The cards should invite improvisation between the three actors. A good card should not expose a flaw in the system. It should create a construction condition that no single actor can solve alone.

## Card Design Principle

The cards are not "random problems." They are prompts for symbiotic collaboration.

A strong card creates all of these:

- a physical condition in the timber assembly;
- a planned action that no longer works;
- a reason the robot is useful;
- a reason the human is still necessary;
- a reason the agent must mediate, sequence, or reinterpret the act.

Avoid cards that make the system look confused, stale, or technically broken. The uncertainty should belong to the construction act, not to the reliability of the interface.

This is why **Blind Corner** and **Stale Plan** are removed from the active deck. They point too much toward system limitation or planning inconsistency. The active cards below focus on material, load, support, danger, and sequencing.

## Draw Structure

Each group draws from three piles.

### 1. Agent Attribute Cards

The group draws 2-3 attributes. These define how the agent behaves during uncertainty. The attributes should sometimes pull against each other.

Example attributes:

- **Patient Mediator:** must ask before overriding human intent.
- **Structural Skeptic:** must question unstable placements before the robot acts.
- **Expressive Collaborator:** must preserve the artistic goal, not only optimize stability.
- **Robot Advocate:** must protect the robot from awkward or overloaded actions.
- **Human Amplifier:** must turn human gestures and material judgement into clear robot tasks.
- **Minimal Speaker:** must use short, precise utterances.
- **Process Narrator:** must keep a spoken trace of what changed and why.
- **Risk-Aware Builder:** must stop the workflow when a beam is unsupported or the load path is not ready.

### 2. Act / Goal Cards

The act defines what must be built. It should describe a spatial intention, not a blueprint.

Example act cards:

- **The Threshold:** build an opening, gate, or passage-like artifact.
- **The Offering Table:** build a small raised platform that can hold a lightweight object.
- **The Signal:** build a directional or pointing structure with a clear orientation.
- **The Leaning Figure:** build a structure that intentionally leans but remains standing.
- **The Bridge:** build a span between two supports.
- **The Shelter:** build a covered or roof-like artifact.
- **The Counterweight:** build something visibly unbalanced that is stabilized by another element.

The act card gives the team a shared purpose. The final object may change, but the group should be able to explain how it still fulfills the act.

### 3. Construction Problem Cards

Problem cards are drawn during the build. Each card introduces uncertainty by invalidating a planned action.

Every card must answer:

- What physical condition changed?
- Which planned action no longer works?
- What must the robot stop doing or change doing?
- What must the human inspect, hold, move, fix, or decide?
- What must the agent say and track?
- How does the artifact change because of the card?

## Five Construction Problem Cards

### Card 1: False Horizon

**Construction problem:** An element must be placed horizontally before any supports are ready.

**When to draw:** Before placing a beam that is meant to span, roof, bridge, or define a level line.

**Physical condition:**

The group must place one 40-50 cm beam horizontally at a chosen height, but the vertical supports are not installed yet. The beam cannot rest by itself.

**Invalidated action:**

The group cannot simply "place the horizontal beam." If the robot releases it, it falls or rotates.

**Collaborative improvisation:**

- Robot becomes a temporary support and holds the beam level.
- Human builds the missing supports underneath the robot-held beam.
- Agent manages the sequence: hold, check level, place first support, place second support, test contact, release slowly.

**Agent intervention:**

> "The horizon beam has no support yet. Robot, hold it level at 32 cm. Human, place the first vertical under the left end. I will not ask for release until both ends have contact."

**Design consequence:**

The final artifact may become lower, narrower, or more scaffold-like because the support structure is built around a beam that first exists as a robot-held line in space.

**Why it matters:**

The card makes the robot temporarily become part of the structure. The human then materializes that temporary geometry with supports.

### Card 2: Broken in Transport

**Construction problem:** One planned beam arrives as two half-length pieces.

**When to draw:** When the group selects a long beam for a span, mast, brace, roof, or cantilever.

**Physical condition:**

The planned 50 cm beam is unavailable as one piece. It is now two 25 cm pieces. The pieces cannot behave as one continuous element unless the team creates a joint, overlap, central support, or split form.

**Invalidated action:**

The group cannot use the element as a single long span or cantilever.

**Collaborative improvisation:**

- Human decides whether the break becomes hidden, supported, or celebrated.
- Robot holds the two pieces aligned while the human builds a support or lap joint.
- Agent keeps the material truth explicit: two half-beams do not carry load like one full beam.

**Agent intervention:**

> "The long beam is now two half-length elements. We can overlap them, support the break from below, or make the split visible as two branches. Which version still serves the act?"

**Design consequence:**

The artifact may become lower, doubled, stepped, forked, or visibly split. The break becomes a formal feature rather than only a failure.

**Why it matters:**

The card forces reinterpretation of the act. The goal survives, but the material logic changes.

### Card 3: Hazard Duty

**Construction problem:** A task enters a no-hand zone, so the robot must perform the risky contact or holding work.

**When to draw:** When a beam must be placed near a pinch point, unstable load, splintered edge, overhead element, wet adhesive, or any zone the human should not touch.

**Physical condition:**

One part of the assembly is treated as dangerous for human contact. Examples:

- the broken beam has a splintered end;
- a heavy beam could pinch fingers while settling;
- a joint has adhesive or a marked "do not touch" surface;
- a beam must be held above hand height while supports are added;
- a wedge must be pressed into a tight gap.

**Invalidated action:**

The human cannot simply hold, press, or guide the dangerous part by hand. The robot must take over the risky physical contact.

**Collaborative improvisation:**

- Robot handles the hazardous end, presses the wedge, holds the heavy beam, or keeps the pinch zone open.
- Human works on the safe side: placing supports, aligning non-hazardous ends, or confirming the design.
- Agent defines the no-hand zone and assigns safe roles.

**Agent intervention:**

> "This end is now a no-hand zone. Robot, take the hazardous end and hold it 5 cm above the joint. Human, work only from the safe side and place the support under the clear end."

**Design consequence:**

The artifact may need a safer support sequence, a wider gap, or a robot-held intermediate state. The robot is not a convenience tool here; it takes the dangerous task so the human can keep designing and fixing from a safer position.

**Why it matters:**

This card expresses a strong HRC idea: the robot contributes by doing the risky physical action, while the human contributes judgement, alignment, and intent.

### Card 4: Unbraced Footing

**Construction problem:** One support will not stand unless it is fixed to another part of the structure.

**When to draw:** After placing a vertical, angled, or leaning support.

**Physical condition:**

One beam used as a post rocks, slides, or tilts when released. Its top moves more than 2 cm, or its base shifts under light pressure. Rotating it is not enough. It must be connected to another beam before it can carry the next load.

**Invalidated action:**

The group cannot continue upward construction on that support. A top beam, crown, span, or cantilever placed on it would fall or twist.

**Collaborative improvisation:**

- Robot holds the unstable support in the intended position.
- Human fixes it to another element with a short brace, wedge, crosspiece, clamp, strap, or improvised timber tie.
- Agent identifies what the support must be fixed to: base rail, neighboring post, diagonal brace, or counterweight.

**Agent intervention:**

> "This footing is not carrying itself. Robot, hold the post at the intended angle. Human, fix its lower end to the base rail with a short brace before we add anything above it."

**Design consequence:**

The artifact gains an explicit fixing element. The brace, tie, or wedge becomes part of the design language, not a hidden repair.

**Why it matters:**

The card forces construction thinking. Stability comes from relationships between parts, not from a single beam standing alone.

### Card 5: Heavy Crown

**Construction problem:** The top element is too heavy for the current support system, so one available element must become a prop or buttress.

**When to draw:** Before placing a top beam, roof, platform, signal arm, crown, or cantilever.

**Physical condition:**

The top element can be lifted by the robot, but the partially built structure cannot carry it with the current supports. When the beam is test-held near the top, the supports lean, twist, or separate.

**Invalidated action:**

The robot cannot release the heavy top element onto the existing structure. Lowering the whole artifact is not the preferred response. The group must create support from the available kit.

**Collaborative improvisation:**

- Robot holds the heavy crown at the intended height or in a safe test position.
- Human adds a prop, second vertical, diagonal strut, or counter-support beneath the expected load.
- Agent names the load path and prevents release until the new support is tested.

**Agent intervention:**

> "The crown is too heavy for the two current supports. Keep the height. Robot, hold the crown in place. Human, use the sixth beam as a prop under the loaded end, then we will test release."

**Design consequence:**

One available element becomes a support, prop, or buttress. The top can remain ambitious, but the structure becomes visibly more supported.

**Why it matters:**

The card avoids simply making the artifact smaller. It asks the trio to preserve the goal by adding structure.

## Additional Card Alternatives

These can expand the deck later.

### Pinch Point

Two beams must be held apart while a third beam is inserted between them. The robot maintains the gap; the human inserts the locking piece; the agent controls the release sequence.

### Missing Twin

The act requires two matching supports, but only one matching beam is available. The group must use asymmetry, a counterweight, or a different support type.

### Twisted Grain

A beam looks straight when unloaded but twists when pressed. The human detects the twist by touch; the robot rotates or holds it; the agent asks whether to use the twist as an expressive feature or brace against it.

### Late Load

After the structure is nearly complete, the act requires it to hold a small object. The group must add support without dismantling the artifact.

## Three Example Scenarios

### Scenario A: The Horizontal Threshold

**Drawn agent attributes:**

- Patient Mediator
- Robot Advocate
- Process Narrator

**Drawn act/goal:**

**The Threshold:** build an opening that reads as a passage or gate.

**Drawn problem cards:**

- False Horizon
- Unbraced Footing

**Starting intention:**

The group wants a small gate: two vertical supports and one horizontal lintel beam. The artifact should look calm and balanced.

**Build sequence:**

1. The human chooses two 35 cm beams as posts and one 45 cm beam as the lintel.
2. The group draws **False Horizon** before the lintel is placed.
3. The agent reframes the lintel as a temporary line held in space.
4. The robot holds the lintel level at 32 cm above the table.
5. The human places the left post under the left end.
6. The human places the right post under the right end, but it rocks when released. This triggers **Unbraced Footing**.
7. The agent blocks release: the right post top moves more than 2 cm.
8. The robot keeps holding the lintel and also lightly steadies the right post.
9. The human fixes the right post to the base rail with a short diagonal brace.
10. The agent asks the human to press and release the braced post once.
11. The post holds. The robot lowers the lintel until both post tops touch it.
12. The robot releases slowly after the agent confirms both ends have contact.

**Concrete uncertainty:**

The uncertainty is not "how do we build a gate?" It is "the horizontal beam has no support yet, and one support cannot carry load until it is fixed to another piece."

**Collaborative effect:**

- Human invents the support fix.
- Robot holds the temporary horizon and stabilizes the weak post.
- Agent sequences the operation and prevents premature release.

**Final artifact:**

A threshold with one visibly braced post. The diagonal fix becomes part of the gate's character.

### Scenario B: The Split Bridge with Hazard Duty

**Drawn agent attributes:**

- Expressive Collaborator
- Structural Skeptic
- Human Amplifier

**Drawn act/goal:**

**The Bridge:** build a span between two supports.

**Drawn problem cards:**

- Broken in Transport
- Hazard Duty

**Starting intention:**

The group wants a continuous 50 cm bridge beam between two low supports. The bridge should feel light and precise.

**Build sequence:**

1. The human places two low supports about 42 cm apart.
2. The group draws **Broken in Transport**.
3. The planned 50 cm span becomes two 25 cm pieces.
4. The agent states that the bridge cannot act as one beam unless the center break is supported or overlapped.
5. The human chooses to make the split visible, with the two halves meeting above a central pier.
6. The human starts to align the two broken ends, but the facilitator marks one splintered end as a no-hand zone. This triggers **Hazard Duty**.
7. The agent assigns the robot to handle the splintered end.
8. The robot holds the hazardous half-beam 3 cm above the center pier.
9. The human places the safe half from the opposite side and adjusts the central support.
10. The robot lowers its half until both pieces meet over the support.
11. The agent asks whether the break should close tightly or remain visible.
12. The human chooses a small visible gap, making the split the bridge's focal point.

**Concrete uncertainty:**

The span breaks into two pieces, and one broken edge cannot be handled by the human. The bridge is only possible if the robot takes the hazardous end while the human builds the safe support condition.

**Collaborative effect:**

- Human redefines the bridge as a split span.
- Robot performs the risky handling task.
- Agent protects both the material truth and the role boundary.

**Final artifact:**

A split bridge with a central support and a visible break line. It still fulfills "bridge," but now as two negotiated halves.

### Scenario C: The Crowned Shelter

**Drawn agent attributes:**

- Risk-Aware Builder
- Expressive Collaborator
- Minimal Speaker

**Drawn act/goal:**

**The Shelter:** build a covered or roof-like artifact.

**Drawn problem cards:**

- Heavy Crown
- Unbraced Footing

**Starting intention:**

The group wants a small pavilion with a heavy roof beam sitting high above two columns. The roof should feel protective and slightly oversized, but the kit has only six timber elements: two triangular base rails, two columns, one crown beam, and one movable strut.

**Build sequence:**

1. The human places the two base rails as a triangular footprint and stands one column.
2. The robot lifts the crown beam and holds it level.
3. The human stands the second column under the opposite end.
4. The group draws **Heavy Crown**.
5. The agent says the two columns alone will not carry the crown safely.
6. The robot keeps the crown at the intended height instead of lowering the concept.
7. The human rotates the sixth element into a diagonal prop from the rear base rail to the underside of the crown.
8. The prop foot slides during the first release test. This triggers **Unbraced Footing**.
9. The robot resumes holding load while the human fixes that same sixth beam into the base rail.
10. The agent names the new load path: crown to diagonal strut, strut to rear base point, crown to the two columns.
11. The human tests the fixed strut by pressing it sideways.
12. The robot releases only after the human confirms the strut no longer shifts.

**Concrete uncertainty:**

The top element is too heavy for the two columns, but there is no seventh piece. The only possible support is the sixth beam. When it becomes a diagonal prop, its foot slides unless it is tied into the triangular base.

**Collaborative effect:**

- Human preserves the oversized roof idea by reinterpreting the sixth beam.
- Robot safely holds the heavy crown during support construction.
- Agent keeps the act from shrinking while enforcing the six-element constraint.

**Final artifact:**

A simple pavilion made from exactly six elements: two triangular base rails, two columns, one crown, and one diagonal strut. The strut shows how the trio protected the act without adding material.

## What Participants Learn

The cards make uncertainty performative and operational.

Participants learn that a collaborative agent is not only a prompt style. It is a behavior pattern for:

- identifying when the construction act has become impossible as planned;
- deciding which actor has the right capability now;
- changing robot autonomy level moment by moment;
- preserving the act/goal even when the structure changes;
- turning physical problems into visible design consequences.

The best final artifacts should look negotiated. A perfect object with no visible trace of uncertainty is less interesting than a strange but legible object shaped by human judgement, robot capability, and agent mediation.
